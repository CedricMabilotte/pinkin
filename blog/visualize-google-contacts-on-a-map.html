How to visualize your Google Contacts on a map

Practical guide · May 2026 · 4 min read

You have hundreds of contacts in Google Contacts. Names, phone numbers, addresses. Useful. But there's no obvious answer to a question that comes up surprisingly often: who lives where?

The Google Contacts UI gives you a list. Search works. But there's no map view. No way to see at a glance which friends are along the route of a road trip, who lives in the neighborhood you're moving to, or which friends drifted to other cities since the last visit.

Existing options, and why most of them are bad

A few services claim to do this. Most have a deal-breaker:

  • Upload your contacts to a third-party server (uncertain privacy policy, no auditable code).
  • Free with ads or a paid tier that gates the map after a few pins.
  • Build your own with Google Sheets + a custom map — fine for one afternoon, painful to maintain.
  • Some browser extensions that haven't been updated since 2019.

What I built: Pinkin

Pinkin is a Chrome extension and a Progressive Web App that does one thing: it puts your Google Contacts as pins on OpenStreetMap.

The non-negotiables. Pinkin has no server (your contacts never leave your browser), no analytics, no third-party calls beyond Google (where your contacts come from) and OpenStreetMap (where the map and geocoding come from). The code is public on GitHub under AGPL-3.0.

How it works, step by step

  1. Install the Chrome extension or open the PWA at pinkin.org/pwa.
  2. Connect to Google Contacts through the OAuth consent screen. Read-only access by default.
  3. Pinkin reads addresses from your contacts and geocodes them via OpenStreetMap Nominatim, one per second (the polite rate Nominatim asks for).
  4. Pins appear on the map. Click one — the contact card opens with their name, photo, phone, email, and one-click buttons to reach them by email, call, SMS, WhatsApp, or Signal.
  5. Optional opt-in: let Pinkin save the geocoded position back into the contact's standard GEO field on Google. Next opening is instant. No re-geocoding.

What Pinkin doesn't do (deliberately)

  • No tracking. No analytics. No telemetry. The phrase "zero tracker" means exactly zero — verifiable by inspecting the source code.
  • No friend graph, no social features, no "people you might know." Just your contacts, on a map.
  • No data leaves your browser except the strictly necessary trips to Google (read your contacts) and OpenStreetMap (render the map).
  • No multi-account, no team sharing. One user, one Google account.

For the privacy-conscious

A common objection: "If the code is open source and the client_secret is in the repo, isn't OAuth compromised?"

Short answer: the OAuth client secret of a "Web" type client running in a browser is de facto public anyway — anyone can extract it from the distributed binary. The real protection is the redirect URI lock-down on Google's side, not the confidentiality of the secret. Full writeup here.
